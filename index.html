A Meal Protocol
for Cognitive Health

Designing a brain-healthy brunch through current science, and traditional wisdom

Sandbox · Winter 2025

The Premise

This meal guide is designed around what modern science and century-old wisdom both tell us: that nourishment begins before the first bite and extends beyond the last.

Every element, from the temperature of the room, the texture and colours of the food, the pace between courses, the quality of our conversation influences how we receive and digest what we eat.

What follows is a protocol, a way of eating that treats cognitive health as something we can practice.

1
01 Social Eating

A 2025 study found that individuals who frequently eat alone have reduced brain volumes in regions critical for cognitive function: the hippocampus and medial temporal lobe.1 The difference disappeared when accounting for diet quality.

In a large population study, solitary eating was associated with reduced brain volume, and the difference was attenuated after accounting for diet quality implying that what (and how well) we eat may partly explain the link.1 Eating with others often nudges us toward more structured meals: we slow down, cook more, and include greater variety, which is consistently associated with better cardiometabolic and brain outcomes.2 And because meals are also social rituals, they reinforce connection and belonging protective factors for long-term cognitive health.3

The Practice

Gather. Make eye contact. Speak about things that matter. The meal is the excuse, the connection is the medicine.

02 The Cephalic Phase

Seeing, smelling, and anticipating food triggers enzyme production and prepares the digestive tract.1 This is called the cephalic phase which is the body's preparation for incoming nourishment.

If we're distracted or rushed, we blunt these cephalic-phase responses, reducing digestive readiness before the first bite.2 Gastric acid, pancreatic enzymes, and bile production all begin in response to sensory cues, not the food itself.3

Engaging the senses before the meal

The Practice

This is why we smell each dish before eating. This is why cooking happens nearby, so the scent reaches the table. This is why we pause to mindfully appreciate the space and the food.

03 Parasympathetic Activation

Digestion only happens fully in "rest and digest" mode.1 When stressed, the sympathetic nervous system triggers "fight or flight" where blood leaves the gut, enzyme production slows, and absorption decreases. The meal passes through without full nourishment.

The vagus nerve is the main channel of parasympathetic activity. Slow breathing with extended exhales directly stimulates vagal tone and shifts the body into digestive readiness.2,3

The Breath Protocol

Inhale through the nose for 4 counts.

Exhale slowly through the mouth for 6-8 counts.

Repeat 5 times. Less than two minutes.

Device surrender

Device Surrender

Phones go into a box. Non-negotiable. The mere presence of a phone on the table — even face down, silent — reduces cognitive capacity and conversation quality.4

04 Fermented Foods

We begin with a small glass of beet kvass and a bite of sauerkraut. The meal also includes lacto-fermented cashew cream and miso.

The gut-brain axis is a bidirectional communication system. About 90-95% of the body's serotonin is produced and stored in the gut (mostly in enterochromaffin cells), though this peripheral serotonin doesn't directly cross the blood-brain barrier.1 The microbiome also modulates inflammation and generates short-chain fatty acids that do cross into the brain.2

Fermented foods introduce beneficial microbes and their metabolites directly. They also contain organic acids, particularly acetic acid, that blunt the glucose response to subsequent foods, stabilising blood sugar, which is itself protective for cognition.3,4,5

Fermented starter (sauerkraut) and steamed beetroot carpaccio

Eat a fermented starter

Start the meal with a ferment for modulating the glucose response and supporting gut microbiome health.

05 Warm Food, Gentle Cooking

All courses are served warm or at room temperature. No cold food. No ice water. This follows the principle practiced at Viva Mayr and in Traditional Chinese Medicine: cold food requires the body to expend energy warming it before digestion can proceed.1

Why we steam instead of roast

High-heat dry cooking creates Advanced Glycation End Products (AGEs) through the Maillard reaction which is the browning that happens during roasting or frying. AGEs accumulate in tissues, increase oxidative stress and inflammation, and are associated with cardiometabolic risk; they are also investigated in neurodegeneration.2,3

We can develop depth through fermentation and time rather than high heat by using elements like miso, black garlic and kombu. This is how Japanese and clinical wellness cuisines build flavour while preserving nutrients and avoiding inflammatory compounds.

Depth through fermentation using miso, not high heat

Cooking Methods

Steaming preserves nutrients and avoids AGE formation.

Blanching keeps vegetables bright and digestible.

Fermented ingredients provide umami without browning or charring.

06 Chewing

Chewing is the only part of digestion under conscious control.1

Digestion begins in the mouth. Salivary amylase starts breaking down starches. Mechanical breakdown increases surface area for enzymes. Chewing also sends signals to the brain that food is being consumed, triggering satiety hormones.

A systematic review found that most studies reported improvements in attention and cognitive performance with chewing.2

Buckwheat: texture that demands chewing mindfully

The Practice

Viva Mayr prescribes 40-60 chews per bite.1

A more reachable goal can be to chew until the food is liquid.

Put down utensils between bites.

The dishes in this menu require chewing like buckwheat, king oyster, nuts, lentils, vegetables.

07 Ingredients (1/2)

Each ingredient is selected for specific cognitive mechanisms.1,2,3

Fermentation

Beetroot (kvass + carpaccio)

Dietary nitrates increase cerebral blood flow4; rich in betalains (antioxidants).

Sauerkraut

A fermented starter: acids + microbial metabolites that can support gut–brain pathways.5

Black Garlic

Deep umami via low-temperature aging; antioxidant activity; flavour without high heat browning.

Miso

Fermented umami; used gently to preserve aromas and fermentation-derived compounds.

Brain-Protective Compounds

Buckwheat

One of the richest dietary sources of rutin (a plant flavonoid). In lab models, rutin (and the related flavonoid quercetin) can interfere with amyloid-β "fibril" formation.6 In an Alzheimer's-model animal study, a rutin-containing Tartary buckwheat fraction improved memory and was associated with higher BDNF expression in the hippocampus.8,11

King Oyster Mushroom

A dietary source of ergothioneine; low plasma ergothioneine is associated with cognitive and functional decline in an older memory-clinic cohort.7

Jerusalem Artichoke

Very high in inulin, a fermentable "prebiotic" fibre. Gut bacteria ferment it to produce SCFAs (short-chain fatty acids: acetate, propionate, and butyrate), supporting gut barrier function and inflammation regulation.9 Jerusalem artichoke is studied specifically for these microbiome and metabolite effects.10

Red Cabbage + Green Beans

Colour + fibre: anthocyanin-rich pigments (red cabbage) and a crisp, low-browning vegetable base (green beans) that stays bright with gentle cooking.

Anthocyanins and fibre in red cabbage, leeks and green blanched green beans

07 Ingredients (2/2)

Neuroprotective Nutrients

Walnuts

Only nut with significant ALA omega-3; also contains polyphenols and melatonin-related compounds.1

Hazelnuts

High in vitamin E, which helps protect neuronal membranes from oxidative damage.2

Pumpkin Seeds

High in zinc, important for neuronal signalling and synaptic function.

Puy Lentils

Protein + fibre base; warm, grounding, and naturally "chewable" (supports satiety and pacing).

Cavolo Nero + Sage

Leafy greens contribute lutein; sage contains compounds that inhibit acetylcholinesterase (a target of some Alzheimer's drugs).3,4

Cavolo nero with pumpkin and black garlic sauce for depth and umami

Herbs & Spices

Herbs and spices matter for two reasons: (1) they intensify aroma, amplifying cephalic-phase signalling (digestive readiness), and (2) they concentrate plant bioactives (polyphenols + volatile compounds) that are widely studied for antioxidant and anti-inflammatory potential.5

Dill + Parsley (chimichurri)

Fresh volatile oils + polyphenols: bright flavour without browning; increases sensory engagement.

Caraway + Black Sesame

Warm, aromatic seeds: deepen flavour while keeping cooking gentle.

Chipotle Flakes + Cinnamon

Warmth as a "digestive cue": spices shape pacing, salivation, and satisfaction with minimal culinary stress.

Polyphenols & Pigments

Dark Chocolate (90%)

Cocoa flavanols are linked to vascular and cognitive outcomes via blood-flow pathways.

Red Cabbage

Anthocyanins (same pigment family as blueberries); a winter-friendly colour anchor.

Tea Timing

Yellow Tea (served after the walk)

Tea polyphenols can inhibit non-heme iron absorption when taken with meals; for anyone managing low iron, the simplest move is to drink tea between meals — which is why we place yellow tea after the walk, at the end.6,7 (Yellow tea is often brewed gently; in practice that may reduce the inhibitory effect compared to very strong brews, but the timing strategy is the reliable lever.)

On Soaking Grains & Legumes

Traditional cultures soaked, sprouted, or fermented grains and legumes before cooking. This can reduce some antinutrients (e.g., phytate) depending on method and time, and may improve mineral bioavailability.8,9,10

Buckwheat: Soak 4-6 hours, rinse, then cook.

Puy lentils: Soak 4-6 hours (or overnight), drain, then boil 20-25 minutes. A full boil is needed to denature lectins.

08 The Invisible Details

Light

Bright natural light from midday, reinforcing circadian message that this is peak metabolic time.1,2 If natural light is insufficient: warm spectrum bulbs (2700K), candles on table.

Temperature

Slightly cool room (18-20°C). The body digests better when not working to cool itself.

Sound

Nature sounds or silence, naturalistic soundscapes are associated with different default-mode and mind-wandering patterns than artificial soundscapes.3

Scent

Cooking happens nearby. Cephalic-phase ("anticipatory") responses begin before eating where smell and anticipation trigger digestive preparation.4

The invisible details in practice

Warm Water

No ice water on the table, warm or room temperature only. Very cold liquids can slow early gastric emptying in some conditions. Defaulting to room-temperature or warm drinks is a simple, low-risk choice.5

Tea Timing

Yellow tea is served after the walk, at the end — partly because tea polyphenols can inhibit non-heme iron absorption when consumed with meals, and "between meals" timing is the simplest mitigation for at-risk groups.6,7

The TLDR; Version

  • Breathe before eating: 5 slow breaths, exhale longer than inhale (4 seconds inhale, 6 seconds exhale)
  • Eat warm food: nothing cold, steamed not roasted
  • Prepare your body for the meal: smell spices and herbs to prime digestion
  • Include fermented foods: kvass, sauerkraut, miso
  • Use herbs & spices: aroma for digestion, depth without browning
  • Chew thoroughly: until liquid
  • Eat with others and be present: conversation, not screens
  • Walk after: 10 minutes or at least 100 steps
  • Tea last: yellow tea after the walk

These changes, consistently applied, can transform digestion and cognition over time.
